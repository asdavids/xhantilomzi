Artist

Xhantilomzi

Voice of the home · Voice of the people

Zuri Dlamini was ten years old when she started classical piano lessons in Soweto. By her teens, she'd found Amapiano — and something clicked that no lesson could have taught her. She named herself Xhantilomzi, isiXhosa for "voice of the home." It's less a stage name than a promise.

Her debut album Umoya Wami ("My Spirit") is the record that promise leads to. Ten tracks and an interlude, moving through love, grief, gratitude, and the particular electricity of a night that refuses to end. She sings in four South African languages — isiZulu, isiXhosa, Tshivenda, Xitsonga — and slips between them mid-song, using language the way other artists use melody: to get somewhere words alone can't reach.

She writes her own songs. She produces. She performs with a live band — keys, bass, two percussionists, and her longtime collaborator DJ/producer Nkosi Moyo — and her shows have been known to stretch a single song past twelve minutes when the room asks for it.

Umoya Wami was recorded at Soweto Sound Studio, mixed in Cape Town, and mastered in London. It sounds like all three at once.
